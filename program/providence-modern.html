Friday, October 10

Providence Modern (1945-1995): Architecture, Design, Planning, Culture

You won’t get a group like this together again! This symposium features presentations from the cultural historians, architects, designers, artists, and independent researchers who know this period of Providence’s design and urban history best. Come for presentations on Ira Rakatansky, Bill Warner, and Friedrich St Florian; modern landmarks like the Providence Post Office (1960) and Hurricane Barrier (1966); and the histories and legacies of urban renewal. The program includes ample time for questions and conversation. Presenters include Edwige Charlot & k. funmilayo aileru, Marisa Angell Brown, Samia Cohen, George Goodwin, Ed Hodges, Michael Kubo, Dietrich Neumann, Mark Rakatansky, Maddock Thomas, Jan Wampler, Christine West, Christopher West, and Jeremy Lee Wolin.

A program for the day, including presentation titles and descriptions, will be available by September 1.

Speaker Bios

This list is continuing to be updated as symposium speakers share their information with us. A program for the day, including presentation titles and descriptions, will be available by September 1.

Marisa Angell Brown is the Executive Director of Providence Preservation Society. She has published articles on modern and contemporary architecture and preservation practice in the Journal of Architectural Education, Places Journal, Perspecta, Buildings and Landscapes, and the Journal of the Society of Architectural Historians. She has a PhD in the History of Art and Architecture from Yale University.

Ed Hodges, AIA, is CEO and Principal of Dimella Shaffer. After spending part of his early career at DiMella Shaffer working under the mentorship of Remmert Huygens, Frank DiMella, Peter Shaffer and others, he returned to the firm in 2001. With the experience gained from working on higher education projects for national design firms, he led the strategic push to expand the higher education practice of the firm. Ed is a Summa Cum Laude graduate of North Carolina State University with degrees in Environmental Design and Architecture.

Michael Kubo’s practice spans the fields of architectural history and theory, architectural design, publishing and curation. He holds a PhD from MIT, where his dissertation focused on the rise and international extension of the architectural corporation after 1945. His work with Chris Grimley on the era of urban renewal has been exhibited in Boston, at the Art Institute of Chicago and at the Heinz Architecture Center at the Carnegie Museum in Pittsburgh. Their work on this topic has been recognized by Docomomo and the Boston Preservation Alliance and featured in The New York Review of Books, The New York Times, The Architect’s Newspaper, The Boston Globe and Metropolis.

Dietrich Neumann is the Christopher Chan and Michelle Ma Professor of the History of Modern Architecture and Urbanism at Brown University. He was trained as an architect in Munich, Germany and at the Architectural Association in London and received his PhD from the Technical University of Munich. His publications have dealt with the history of skyscrapers, movie set design, architectural illumination, building materials and in particular with the work of Ludwig Mies van der Rohe. He is a member of the Committee on Architecture and Design at the Museum of Modern Art and currently serves as president of Docomomo New England.

Mark Rakatansky is an Adjunct Associate Professor in the Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation at Columbia University, and principal of Mark Rakatansky Studio. He is the author of Tectonic Acts of Desire and Doubt (2012) and the forthcoming Signifying Media: The Imprinting of Palladio (2026). Grants and Fellowships include the Chicago Institute for Architecture and Urbanism, the Graham Foundation for Advanced Studies in the Fine Arts, Elise Jaffe + Jeffrey Brown, The New York State Council on the Arts, and the Samuel H. Kress Fellowship from The James Marston Fitch Foundation.

Maddock Thomas is a senior in the urban studies program at Brown University with an interest in the relationship between design and the evolution of the 20th century urban landscape. He has published extensively on railroads and public transit from both a historical and policy perspective and serves as the President of the Brown Labor Council. His honors thesis focuses on the Providence Post Office where labor, politics, design, and transportation themes collide.

Jan Wampler received his Bachelor of Science in Architecture at the Rhode Island School of Design in 1963 and his Master of Architecture in Urban Design from the Harvard Graduate School of Design in 1964. He was a Professor of Architecture at the MIT School of Architecture and has been a Visiting Professor at several universities around the world, including the University of California at Berkeley, the University of Sydney in Australia,and Tsinghua University in Beijing, China. Wampler is a Fellow of the American Institute of Architects and was awarded the Distinguished Professor honor from the Association of Collegiate Schools of Architecture.

Dr. Christopher West is the Executive Director of the African American Museum of Rhode Island, a community-based initiative that preserves history of the African Diaspora in the 20th and 21st centuries with a focus on workforce development. AAMRI uniquely creates a space for youth to research, preserve and tell Black stories.
