Providence Modern (1945-1995): Architecture, Design, Planning, Culture

You won’t get a group like this together again! This symposium features presentations from the cultural historians, architects, designers, artists, and independent researchers who know this period of Providence’s design and urban history best. Come for presentations on Ira Rakatansky, Bill Warner, and Friedrich St Florian; modern landmarks like the Providence Post Office (1960) and Hurricane Barrier (1966); and the histories and legacies of urban renewal. The program includes ample time for questions and conversation. Presenters include Edwige Charlot & k. funmilayo aileru, Marisa Angell Brown, Samia Cohen, George Goodwin, Ed Hodges, Michael Kubo, Dietrich Neumann, Mark Rakatansky, Maddock Thomas, Jan Wampler, Christine West, Christopher West, and Jeremy Lee Wolin.

Presentations

Lynnette Widder

Son of an immigrant Russian contractor and nephew of a skilled seamstress, Ira Rakatansky arrived at Harvard's GSD with deep understanding of how things were made. Through his studies with Walter Gropius and Marcel Breuer, for whom he worked as a first American employee, Rakatansky developed a strong spatial sensibility for the openness of Modern architecture. Working for himself in Providence after graduation, he combined his smarts with a vision of contemporary life to realize a series of unorthodox and beautiful small houses.

Maddock Thomas

Built as the first automated post office in 1959-1960 and designed by C. A. Maguire, the Providence Post Office was constructed through a novel leaseback agreement between the Postal Department and I.T.T., the private company that owned and constructed the facility. It served not only as a technological testament to the future and political chess piece in the 1960 election, but also as the center piece of the urban renewal project known as the West River Industrial Park.

Mark Rakatansky

In William Jordy’s Buildings of Rhode Island (2004) the sole citation of Ira Rakatansky was by name only, merely listing his addition to the Brown Faculty Club. Twenty years later—subsequent to the 2010 publication of John Caserta and Lynnette Widder’s Ira Rakatansky: As Modern as Tomorrow, the acquisition of his archive by his alma mater Harvard University, tours of his work by DOCOMOMO and the SAH—he has been cited in Katy Picken’s PPS Cityscape article “Providence Modern: The Work of Ira Rakatansky” as “arguably Providence’s preeminent modernist.” And yet, seemingly now no longer hidden in plain sight, the innovations of his Rhode Island modernism may still have more to reveal beyond their seemingly plain-sighted clean modernist lines, which should, as Dorothy Agnew’s 1957 Providence Sunday Journal’s feature article’s title foretold, make us “look twice.”

Samia Cohen

Completed in 1966, the Fox Point Hurricane Barrier (FPHB) is seldom mentioned in accounts of downtown Providence’s waterfront revival. And yet, without a giant dam to keep it dry, the inner harbor of the Providence River would almost certainly not have become the locus of placemaking and public investment that it has been for the last forty years. This presentation will explore the making of the FPHB, its complex legacy, and the inner harbor’s possible futures.

Marisa Brown and Jan Wampler

Wampler was a young RISD student when he went to work for Bill Warner in the early 1960s. In this conversation, Wampler recalls his time working in Warner’s office during this period and what architecture and planning culture was like in the city well before its “Renaissance.”

Katy, Keating and B.L Jordan

This presentation explores how Providence's public housing developments were architectural expressions of modernist ideology and instruments of racial segregation. As urban renewal cleared entire neighborhoods throughout the city in the mid-20th century, where did these displaced residents go? We follow a handful of families from redevelopment areas, looking to understand the systematic relocation of non-white, lower-income families to public housing. Through case studies of specific Providence housing developments and the communities they replaced, this talk examines how public housing policy combined with modernist planning and design transformed the city's landscape.

Christopher West

In the post-WWII era, Providence, RI became home to rich African Diasporic communities. Merchant marine and the whaling Cape Verdean communities made their homes in Fox Point. Laborers, agricultural workers, and entrepreneurs from the southern United States as well as West African and Afro-Caribbean migrants that clustered around Prairie Avenue and Lippitt Hill. Federal, state, and city policy flattened these communities into the bulk shorthand "Negro," at the same time as these populations continued to expand in the 1970s. Urban renewal policies had a sweeping effect on the availability of public and private housing to Black communities, resulting in multigenerational loss of individual real property and in aggregate community power. This presentation weaves and disaggregates the term "Negro" by utilizing cartography and objects to narrate stories of community loss in the urban landscape.

Edwige and funmi

The South Providence People’s Archive (SPPA) is an emerging collection commemorating the Black and Native American legacies and lineages of South Providence (RI) – past and present. The archive’s co-founders, Edwige Charlot and k. funmilayo aileru, will present their relationship-based process of bringing intimate accounts of Black and Native American life to the public record.

Claire Andrade-Watkins and Michael Pereira

Claire and Michael will talk about the vanguard work of the Tockwotton Fox Point Cape Verdean Project team, and how “they leverage 21st-century digital technology to preserve, maintain, and create a digital footprint of the living but physically erased history of Rhode Island’s first Cape Verdean neighborhood.” (2022 Most Endangered Property, Providence Preservation Society), downthepoint.com.

Michael Kubo

This talk explores a series of global currents in architecture culture that converged in Providence's design scene in the early 1970s, particularly around RISD and Brown. This story focuses on the important but little-known Architecture 1962-1972 exhibition at the newly-opened List Arts Center at Brown, which featured the work of four international avant garde architects who all taught at RISD in these years: Adolfo Natalini of Superstudio, Michael Webb of Archigram, Friedrich St. Florian, and Michael Webb.

Ed Hodges

The Dr. William Mauran House, often characterized as brutalist, and appears to contrast to its neighborhood context. In this session, we will learn, in an interesting twist, that the real drivers of the design were the site and context, along with the owner’s desire for a modern residence.

Christine West

Using the recently completed Summer Street Apartments, KITE Principal Christine West, AIA will describe how KITE Architects’ design approach is neither “traditional” nor “modern”. It offers a new path forward that centers our needs as humans blended with a rigorous approach to craft. This viewpoint is central to understanding the unique urban character of the largest supportive housing complex in New England.

Speaker Bios

This list is continuing to be updated as symposium speakers share their information with us. A program for the day, including presentation titles and descriptions, will be available by September 1.

k. funmilayo aileru (pronouns: xi/they) is an artist and cultural bearer who speculates what Indigenous sovereignty and Black liberation might look like beyond our colonial conditions. xyr work centers access to transformative art experiences, accountability in design processes, and Indigenous craft traditions. aileru lives and works on xyr ancestral, Narragansett–Niantic homelands alongside their partner, child, community, culture, and creation.

Filmmaker/Historian Claire Andrade-Watkins, PhD, filmmaker/historian, is the Director of the Tockwotton Fox Point Cape Verdean Heritage Project, Inc., President of SPIA Media Productions, Inc., and a second-generation descendant of the first community of Cape Verdeans in Rhode Island who settled in the Fox Point section of Providence, RI, at the beginning of the 20th century. Claire’s career as a distinguished scholar of colonial and post-colonial francophone and lusophone African cinema spans decades. Her trajectory as an award-winning Director and filmmaker focuses on the Tockwotton Fox Point Cape Verdean community in Rhode Island and the Global Cape Verdean Diaspora.

Marisa Angell Brown is the Executive Director of Providence Preservation Society. She has published articles on modern and contemporary architecture and preservation practice in the Journal of Architectural Education, Places Journal, Perspecta, Buildings and Landscapes, and the Journal of the Society of Architectural Historians. She has a PhD in the History of Art and Architecture from Yale University.

Edwige Charlot (pronouns: they/them) is a French-born immigrant artist of Haitian ancestry. As a descendant of the homeisland of Ayiti, Charlot uses nature-based motifs to grapple with Caribbean and Creole identities and culture. Charlot’s work with the public and community-based arts organizations envelops collaborations across New England, including a commission by the Fruitlands Museum (Harvard, MA), the Pleasant Street Park mural (Portland, ME) and, recently, a commission by the Providence Commemoration Lab.

Samia Cohen (she or they) is a writer, editor, teacher, public scholar, and creative practitioner with an interest in environmental storytelling, urban environmental history, and transgender studies. She is at work on a book manuscript, titled Impermeable: Stories of A Landfilled Urban Coast, that explores the hydrological legacy of Providence, Rhode Island, where Samia was born, and the life that free-flowing water has supported in the city across time.

Ed Hodges is the CEO and a Principal at DiMella Shaffer and brings over three decades of architectural experience to his work in the housing and higher education sectors. Guided by a belief that blends artistic vision with practical insight, he leads the firm with purpose and a deep respect for the craft of architecture. A storyteller at heart, in 2015 Ed wrote nearly every week for a year, chronicling the 50-year history of DiMella Shaffer in the Building Conversations blog.

Michael Kubo is an associate professor in the department of architecture at the Rhode Island School of Design. His practice spans the fields of architectural history and theory, advocacy and historic preservation, and curatorial and publishing practices. His co-authored books on twentieth-century architecture, urbanism, and disciplinary discourse include Futures of the Architectural Exhibition(2022), Imagining the Modern: Architecture and Urbanism of the Pittsburgh Renaissance (2019), Heroic: Concrete Architecture and the New Boston (2015), and OfficeUS Atlas (2015).

Dietrich Neumann is the Christopher Chan and Michelle Ma Professor of the History of Modern Architecture and Urbanism at Brown University. He was trained as an architect in Munich, Germany and at the Architectural Association in London and received his PhD from the Technical University of Munich. His publications have dealt with the history of skyscrapers, movie set design, architectural illumination, building materials and in particular with the work of Ludwig Mies van der Rohe. He is a member of the Committee on Architecture and Design at the Museum of Modern Art and currently serves as president of Docomomo New England.

Mike Pereira was born and raised in the Fox Point neighborhood of Providence, Rhode Island. He was a founding team member and served as COO of Digital Jungle, a cross-cultural marketing company based in Beijing. He’s a former World Bank consultant and starting team member of the Development Gateway (created within and spun off from the World Bank) where he served as Director of Global Online Communities. Previously he was Senior Project Manager and Online Business Consultant at Scholastic Inc. (NASDAQ: SCHL), where he helped to launch Scholastic Network, the first nationwide web-based online K-8 educational platform. Mike is a graduate of Boston University and the Harvard Kennedy School of Government.

Katy Pickens is a researcher and writer for the Providence Preservation Society. She graduated from Brown University with a degree in Urban Studies and Environmental Studies. Katy has been writing and reporting on Providence since 2021, and is passionate about housing and planning policy, storytelling, and community preservation.

Mark Rakatansky is an Adjunct Associate Professor in the Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation at Columbia University, and principal of Mark Rakatansky Studio. He is the author of Tectonic Acts of Desire and Doubt (2012) and the forthcoming Signifying Media: The Imprinting of Palladio (2026). Grants and Fellowships include the Chicago Institute for Architecture and Urbanism, the Graham Foundation for Advanced Studies in the Fine Arts, Elise Jaffe + Jeffrey Brown, The New York State Council on the Arts, and the Samuel H. Kress Fellowship from The James Marston Fitch Foundation.

Maddock Thomas is a senior in the urban studies program at Brown University with an interest in the relationship between design and the evolution of the 20th century urban landscape. He has published extensively on railroads and public transit from both a historical and policy perspective and serves as the President of the Brown Labor Council. His honors thesis focuses on the Providence Post Office where labor, politics, design, and transportation themes collide.

Jan Wampler received his Bachelor of Science in Architecture at the Rhode Island School of Design in 1963 and his Master of Architecture in Urban Design from the Harvard Graduate School of Design in 1964. He was a Professor of Architecture at the MIT School of Architecture and has been a Visiting Professor at several universities around the world, including the University of California at Berkeley, the University of Sydney in Australia,and Tsinghua University in Beijing, China. Wampler is a Fellow of the American Institute of Architects and was awarded the Distinguished Professor honor from the Association of Collegiate Schools of Architecture.

Dr. Christopher West is the Executive Director of the African American Museum of Rhode Island, a community-based initiative that preserves history of the African Diaspora in the 20th and 21st centuries with a focus on workforce development. AAMRI uniquely creates a space for youth to research, preserve and tell Black stories.

Lynnette Widder is Professor of Practice at Columbia University and author of three books on architecture. Her architectural design work with aardvarchitecture and on her own has been widely published. Her recent articles appear New German Critique and Oxonian Review, Urban Omnibus, Dimensions and Materia.

Jeremy Lee Wolin is a PhD candidate in History and Theory of Architecture at Princeton University who studies the impact of race, design, and the state on architectures of social welfare. His current project tracks how struggles over the construction of spaces for health, education, employment, and social services articulated the terms of minority citizenship in the post-civil rights era. His work has appeared in Planning Perspectives, the Journal of the Society of Architectural Historians, the Journal of Asian American Studies, and Metropole. Previously, he taught at Pratt Institute and received a BA from Brown University and a BFA from Rhode Island School of Design.

Keating Zelenke is a reporter-researcher for The Providence Post, a newsletter from the Providence Preservation Society. Before moving to Rhode Island, she received national recognition as a two-time finalist for the Hearst Journalism Awards. As the daughter of a carpenter, she has an interest in understanding the places we call home.
